üìñ What are Conditional Sentences?

Conditional sentences express a condition (the "if" part) and its result (the "then" part). They describe what happens IF something else happens. There are 4 main types in English, but here we focus on the most common: Type 0 (Zero Conditional) and Type 1 (First Conditional).

üü¢ Type 0: Zero Conditional (Universal Truths)

The Zero Conditional is used for general truths, scientific facts, and situations that are always true. The result is inevitable and doesn't depend on time. It describes what ALWAYS happens in certain conditions.

‚öôÔ∏è IF + PRESENT SIMPLE, PRESENT SIMPLE

Structure:

IF clause (condition): If + Present Simple
Main clause (result): Present Simple

Example: IF you heat water to 100¬∞C, it boils.

Examples:

1. Scientific fact:
"If you mix red and blue paint, you get purple."
‚úì Always true - general fact
2. Natural law:
"If it rains, the ground gets wet."
‚úì Always true - natural consequence
3. General routine:
"If you don't eat breakfast, you feel hungry by noon."
‚úì Always happens - general experience
4. Instructions/Rules:
"If you press this button, the door opens."
‚úì Always happens - mechanical result
üåü KEY FEATURES OF TYPE 0:
‚úì Describes UNIVERSAL TRUTHS
‚úì Always/usually happens
‚úì Scientific facts or general knowledge
‚úì Present Simple in BOTH parts
‚úì Condition = certain result
‚úì Time is NOT important (not about future)

üîµ Type 1: First Conditional (Possible Future)

The First Conditional is used for real, possible situations in the future. It describes what WILL happen IF something happens. The condition is realistic and the outcome is probable.

‚öôÔ∏è IF + PRESENT SIMPLE, WILL + INFINITIVE

Structure:

Main clause (result): Will + Base Verb

1. Future prediction:
"If it rains tomorrow, I will stay home."
‚úì Possible future situation
2. Warning:
"If you don't study, you will fail the test."
‚úì Realistic consequence of action
3. Promise/Offer:
"If you help me, I will help you later."
‚úì Condition with promised result
4. Consequence:
"If you arrive late, the teacher will be angry."
‚úì Real possible situation in future
üåü KEY FEATURES OF TYPE 1:
‚úì Describes POSSIBLE FUTURE situations
‚úì Realistic and likely to happen
‚úì Present Simple in IF clause
‚úì Will + Infinitive in main clause
‚úì Condition = probable result
‚úì Time is IMPORTANT (future focused)

üìù Important Notes

‚≠ê IF vs WHEN:

IF = uncertain, may or may not happen
"IF it rains, I will bring an umbrella." (Maybe it will rain)

WHEN = certain, will definitely happen
"WHEN it rains, I will bring an umbrella." (It definitely will rain)
‚≠ê WORD ORDER:

‚úì "If you study, you will pass." (IF clause first)
‚úì "You will pass if you study." (IF clause second)

NOTE: When IF clause is first, use a comma.
‚≠ê OTHER POSSIBLE RESULTS:

Instead of WILL, you can use:
‚Ä¢ MIGHT/MAY (possible): "If you study hard, you might get an A."
‚Ä¢ CAN (ability): "If you practice, you can improve."
‚Ä¢ IMPERATIVE (command): "If you see him, tell him I'm here."
